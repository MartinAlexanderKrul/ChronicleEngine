# 1. The Gatefall

Ten years ago the sky did not change and the ground did not shake. Gates simply appeared — matte, edgeless apertures that hung in the air over parking lots, in the middle of streets, inside office lobbies and forests and once in the nave of a cathedral. They opened on every continent inside nine days. People walked up to the first ones and were killed by what walked out.

A Gate is a mouth. Behind it is a **pocket dungeon**: a bounded interior with its own light and weather, filled with **magic beasts** that exist nowhere in nature and answer to no biology anyone can name. In that same first week a sliver of humanity **awakened** — woke one morning with mana in their bodies, strength or speed or senses no ordinary person has, and a rank the measuring machines would soon learn to read. The two events were never separated in anyone's mind. The Gates and the awakened arrived together, and the world has spent a decade deciding they must belong to each other.

The first casualties were catastrophic because nobody knew the rules. A Gate left standing past its timer **breaks** — its interior empties into the street. Whole blocks were lost before anyone understood that a Gate has a clock, that only the awakened can enter, that killing the thing at the bottom shuts the mouth for good. The rules were paid for in bodies. They are common knowledge now because everyone alive remembers what it cost to learn them.

# 2. The Rules Everyone Knows

These are not theories. They are the settled facts a ten-year-old can recite, printed on the back of every evacuation card.

- **Gates open without warning** and hang in place. Detection is by mana signature; response is legally the government's before it is anyone's.
- **Only the awakened can enter.** A Gate's threshold turns ordinary people away like a wall; the awakened pass through it.
- **Every Gate has a timer.** From the moment a Gate is detected it runs a clock — about a week for the smallest, two days for the worst. The timer is read from the Gate's mana pressure and is never exact, which is why grading carries confidence, not certainty.
- **A Gate left past its timer breaks.** The interior population pours into the physical world at the Gate's location. A break is graded like the Gate that caused it: an E-break is a bad afternoon; an A-break is a national emergency.
- **Killing the boss closes the Gate.** Every dungeon has one apex threat at the bottom. Kill it and the mouth collapses safely, the interior gone. There is no other reliable way to shut a Gate; you clear it or you contain the break.
- **Awakening is rare, involuntary, and permanent.** It cannot be chosen, bought, or induced. Most people never awaken. Those who do keep their mana and their abilities for life.
- **Rank is measured once and never changes.** An awakened is assessed by their mana capacity and placed on the ladder **E, D, C, B, A, S**. The number is fixed at awakening. There is no promotion, no leveling, no growth past your measured ceiling. An E-rank dies an E-rank; an A-rank is born an A-rank. This is the single hardest law in the world, and everyone believes it without exception.
- **Mana is measurable.** Standardized appraisal cells read an awakened's capacity in the field. The same cells grade Gates and grade loot, all on the one E-through-S band, so a monster, a hunter, and a sword can be spoken of in the same breath.
- **Loot is real and it is the economy.** Beasts leave **mana crystals**; elites and bosses leave **cores**. These sell for money. Everything else a Gate yields — gear, potions, teaching-stones — is graded on the same band and traded on an open market.

# 3. Hunter Scale

The population pyramid is fixed world-canon so that "how rare is a hunter?" is never improvised. The numbers below are canonical (Anchor A9), copied verbatim:

> awakening ≈ 1 in 5,000 (~1.6M worldwide); licensed-hunter rank split E 60% / D 22% / C 11% / B 5% / A ~2%; S counted individually — ~200 worldwide, 11 US (2 in Chicago), 1 Czech. Chicago ~1,200 licensed; Prague ~350.

Filled out at national depth:

- **Worldwide:** roughly **1.6 million awakened**; a minority hold hunter licenses. About **200 S-ranks** exist on Earth, each a strategic national asset counted by name, not by percentage.
- **United States:** ~**66,000 awakened**, ~**25,000 licensed hunters**, **11 S-ranks**. **Chicago:** ~**1,200 licensed hunters**, **2 resident S-ranks** — Horizon Guild's ace, and one BGM federal asset, **Elias Kane**, who answers to Washington before the city.
- **Czech Republic:** ~**2,100 awakened**, ~**800 licensed hunters**, **1 S-rank** — a national celebrity and a permanent political football. **Prague:** ~**350 licensed hunters**, small enough that every B-rank is a known name and a new face is noticed within a week. That intimacy is the whole point of starting there.

**Rank in one line each, as the trade uses them:**
- **E** — newly licensed; clears E-Gates in supervised parties. The floor, and where most hunters stay.
- **D** — competent; solo E-work, D-Gate team member. The working majority above the floor.
- **C** — experienced; the backbone of a serious raid party.
- **B** — veteran; rare enough that in Prague every one is known by name.
- **A** — elite; a public figure, courted by every major guild.
- **S** — off the scale; a person the size of a policy. Cannot be compelled by any institution; only another S-rank can physically oppose one.

**What non-hunter awakened do.** Most awakened never take a hunter license, and the world runs on them as much as on the raiders. Awakened bodies and senses are worth more outside the Gates than a low license pays inside them:
- **Appraisers** read mana in the field — grading Gates, grading loot, testing new awakened. The whole grading system rests on them.
- **Construction and industry** hire high-Strength and high-Vitality awakened for work no machine matches; crystal-and-core refining is an awakened trade.
- **Medicine** uses awakened with restorative or perception gifts as diagnosticians and emergency responders; a Perception-awakened triage nurse is worth a ward.
- **Military and police** field awakened units, though the good ones are constantly poached by guilds.
- **Smuggling and the black market** run on awakened who can carry, hide, or move contraband cores and artifacts — the shadow labor force under the licensed economy.

A licensed hunter is therefore a specific, self-selected minority: the awakened who chose the most dangerous way to use what they have.

**Working types.** Abilities do not sort by rank alone; the trade also reads them by *what they do*, and four working types are recognized on every guild board. **Strikers** deal damage and make up the bulk of any roster. **Wardens** are barrier-and-frontline: they hold a line and take the hit for the party. **Menders** heal by touch, closing wounds in the middle of a fight. **Sensors** read the field — ambush, anomaly, and a Gate's true grade — the appraisal-adjacent gift turned toward combat. Strikers and sensors are common; **wardens and menders are rare, on the order of one awakened in twelve.** Scarcity sets the wage: a C-rank mender out-earns a B-rank striker, and a competent mender or warden coming free touches off a quiet bidding war among guilds, the pay running well past what the same rank commands on the front line. A hired mender's session prices near a same-grade beast core, and mender care counts as professional care for injury recovery (profile Section 12.7, Section 6.3). A serious C-Gate party wants all four types under contract; most E- and D-rank parties go without and pay for the gap in the injuries they take. At the very top the lines blur — an S-rank's power is usually too total to file under a single working type — but through the working ranks, the four types are how the trade actually staffs a Gate.

## The World Ranking

S-rank is the top of the fixed ladder, but it is not flat. Of the ~200 S-ranks alive, ten are **world-ranked** — individually numbered, one through ten, on a single global list the **International Gate Registry (IGR)** publishes and revises. The IGR is the Geneva-based inter-agency clearinghouse the national agencies and the European Gate Council all feed their verified clearance records into; the World Ranking is its most-read product and, unofficially, the most argued-over document in the profession. A world-ranked hunter is a household name on every continent — a de facto tier above plain S, spoken of the way the last century spoke of heavyweight champions.

**What the number is built on.** The IGR ranks on two measured inputs and nothing else it will admit to: a hunter's **verified clearance record** (grade and difficulty of Gates closed, audited against agency filings) and their **measured output ceiling** (the peak mana throughput the appraisal cells read at assessment). Everything else — reputation, media, a nation's lobbying — is supposed to stay out of it, and everyone knows some of it gets in anyway.

**It is contested, and the contest is political.** Placements are disputed constantly: opaque, militarized states (Russia, China) file almost nothing, so their entries are IGR *estimates* carried under standing objection, suspected by rivals of being either padded or under-read. Agencies lobby for their own nationals. And the American slot is the sorest of all — **no world-ranked hunter has ever held the American #1 billing without dispute**, because the United States has more to prove and a habit of proving it with staged, federally-assigned clears that critics say inflate the record. The list is real, the top handful are genuinely uncontested, and the seams below that are where the arguing lives.

**Below the ten.** An S-rank outside the ten is still off the human scale — a strategic national asset — but is not "world-ranked" and does not carry a number. The Czech Republic's Tereza Blažková (Section 6, `220_NOTABLE_FIGURES.md`) sits somewhere in the low teens on the IGR list: nationally seismic, the single fact Czech Gate policy is built around, and globally a mid-S hunter who has never cracked the ten. The two resident S-ranks of Chicago are likewise plain S, unnumbered, and known worldwide only to the trade.

**The current ten**, as the trade recites them (rank · name · country · guild/affiliation · signature · who they are):

1. **Seo Jae-won** · South Korea · Hanul Guild, Seoul · *Ninth Blade*, a stance-dueling swordsmanship no other S-rank has beaten in single combat · the undisputed world #1, a recluse who clears alone, endorses nothing, and is the one placement no agency contests.
2. **Kaito Arima** · Japan · Tenryū Association, Osaka · *Thunderfold*, a speed-strike cascade that ends a fight in the opening exchange · the oldest world-ranked hunter still active and the doctrine-author whose training manuals the rest of the world drills from.
3. **Mikhail Ostrovsky** · Russia · state hunter corps (no guild) · *Deadweight*, a gravity-suppression field that pins a whole chamber to the floor · Russia releases only what flatters him, so the IGR carries his rank under a permanent dispute over an unverifiable clearance record.
4. **Zhao Wenqing** · China · state Gate service (no publicly traded guild) · *Cinnabar Ward*, a talismanic zoning discipline that turns a dungeon's own geometry against it · China publishes almost nothing, making her placement the IGR's single most-contested estimate — Western agencies quietly believe she is under-ranked.
5. **Imogen Hart** · United Kingdom · Zenith Group, London · *Arrears*, a mark-and-collect technique that defers a strike's full damage to a moment of her choosing · Zenith's marquee poach, acquired at a fee that reset the S-rank market and the boardroom's proof its chequebook strategy works.
6. **Camille Aubrac** · France · Lumière Guild, Paris · *Prism Veil*, light-refraction that blinds and scatters a whole pack at once · a telegenic hunter who turned a world ranking into an endorsement empire — admired by the public and resented inside the trade.
7. **Reid Calloway** · United States · Keystone Guild, New York · *Overrun*, an escalating force-discipline that out-damages anything it can reach · the highest-ranked American and the most disputed name in the ten (full entry in `220_NOTABLE_FIGURES.md`).
8. **Lukas Reinhardt** · Germany · Brandhof, Frankfurt · *Bastion*, a fortress-summoning barrier-and-siege discipline that walls a break and grinds it down · Brandhof's flagship and the nearest world-ranked hunter to Prague (full entry in `220_NOTABLE_FIGURES.md`).
9. **Arjun Nair** · India · Ashoka Collective, Mumbai · *Ashfall*, an area-denial of searing motes that attritions a swarm to nothing · rose through the world's largest under-served Gate market and carries a subcontinent's expectations as its first world-ranked hunter.
10. **Beatriz Fontes** · Brazil · Cruzeiro Guild, São Paulo · *Undertow*, a momentum-reversal grappling discipline that turns a charge into a throw · a São Paulo folk hero whose ranking is a national cause, and the newest name on the list.

# 4. The United States

Gate affairs are federally regulated by the **Bureau of Gate Management (BGM)**, a Cabinet-adjacent agency with the reach of the FAA and the temperament of the SEC. The BGM does four things and guards each jealously:

- **Licensing.** Every awakened must register; anyone who enters a Gate must be licensed. Unlicensed entry is a federal felony. Licensing is slow, tiered, and paperwork-heavy — which is exactly why so much of hunter life happens at the edges of it. In the Region V office that covers Chicago, the backlog runs **six to ten weeks** from intake to license.
- **Grading.** BGM appraisers assess each detected Gate for grade and timer and publish a **confidence** with the grade. A low-confidence grade is a legal hedge and a death trap: the Gate may be worse than its label, and mis-graded Gates are how low-rank hunters die.
- **Contract auctions.** The BGM does not clear Gates. It **auctions the clearance contract** — the right and the obligation to close a given Gate before its timer — to licensed guilds and freelancers. Auction cadence, payout by grade, and loot-declaration rules are all federal; Region V runs its clearance auction **every business day**.
- **Enforcement.** Undeclared loot, black-market core sales, and unlicensed entry are the BGM's to prosecute. It is chronically overstretched and picks its battles.

**Guild capitalism.** Clearing is private industry. The **majors are publicly traded corporations** with balance sheets, investor calls, and **drafted S-ranks** whose contracts are reported like sports trades. Below them, thousands of freelance E- and D-ranks work the small contracts the majors don't bother with. The world adapted the way the real world always adapts: there are Gate-risk insurance markets, a crystal commodities exchange, cable-news raid coverage, guild sponsorship deals, and hunter agents who take a percentage.

**Who buys the loot, and why.** The crystal-and-core economy has a real industrial demand side, not just a speculative one — the loot is priced because industry needs it. **Mana crystals are feedstock.** They are refined into power storage and generation and into the mana-active materials that the whole **mana-tech** sector is built from: appraisal cells, cordon and detection gear, field instruments, and high-end medical hardware all begin as refined crystal. Two corporate refiners dominate the licensed buy-side and set the floor under every posted crystal price — the American **Cindermark Refining** and the European multinational **Halcyon Mana-Industrial** — and their appetite is what the commodities exchange actually trades on. **Beast cores are the enchanting input**: the graded gear the trade fights with is forged from cores, so an armory's supply of B-grade weapons is exactly the elites and bosses hunters bring down, and the enchanting industry lives or dies by core flow. Refined **B-and-above crystal is a strategic commodity** — the BGM and the European agencies run **export controls** on B+ grades, treating high-grade mana-tech feedstock as the dual-use material it is, and the running fight over who may ship it where is one of the quieter but sharper axes of Gate-era politics.

**Chicago's position.** Chicago is a top-five US Gate city. Gate density runs high along the lakefront and the L corridors, a geography the trade has learned to read like weather. The city fields **two major guilds** — the top-tier, publicly traded **Horizon Guild**, and its blue-collar rival **Ironline Guild**, an aggressive recruiter of low-ranks who sells itself as the working hunter's guild. Freelancers organize through the **Windy City Hunters Coalition**, a co-op that pools contracts, shares intel, and gives the unaffiliated a floor to stand on — the likeliest first home for a hunter with no name yet. Over all of it sits the **BGM Region V office**, the federal presence that grades the Gates, runs the auctions, and decides which incidents become investigations.

# 5. Europe and the World

Europe runs Gate affairs through **national agencies coordinated by the European Gate Council** — a coordination body, not a super-regulator, which is the source of most of its friction. Each nation keeps its own agency and its own pride; the Council standardizes what it can and argues about the rest. Its one unambiguous achievement is **license reciprocity**: a hunter licensed in any member state may work in all of them. That single treaty made Europe's hunter economy transnational overnight.

**Guild strength scales with home-country size and Gate density.** A guild is only as strong as the awakened population and Gate flow it can draw on, so large, dense countries field top-tier guilds that operate across borders, and small countries cannot sustain a domestic guild at S-rank tier no matter how proud they are. Small nations therefore **import clearance capacity** — and live with the standing political tension between sovereignty and safety, between clearing your own Gates and letting a foreign corporation do it on your soil.

The major internationals operating across Europe, one line each:

- **Brandhof** — German; the continent's largest guild. An engineering-corps culture: disciplined, methodical, treats a Gate clear like a construction project with a critical path. Where Brandhof bids, it usually wins on competence.
- **Zenith Group** — British; finance-driven. Zenith **buys rather than builds** — it acquires guilds, poaches S-ranks with money no one else will match, and treats hunters as an asset class. Feared in boardrooms more than in Gates.
- **Horizon Guild — Europe** — the American major's European arm: well-funded, resented as a foreign intrusion, and steadily hiring away local talent city by city. Its Prague branch is the thread that stitches Chicago and Prague into one world.

**East Asia.** The densest Gate region on Earth and the most mature hunter culture. Japan and South Korea produced early S-ranks and export doctrine, training methods, and gear the rest of the world copies; their agencies are well-funded and their guilds disciplined. China runs a state-controlled model — no publicly traded guilds, hunters as national servants — and publishes almost nothing, which unsettles everyone downwind of its numbers.

**The rest of the world, in brief.** Where states are strong the licensed model holds; where they are weak, Gates go uncleared and breaks are simply endured, and the map of who dies to Gates tracks the map of who was already poor. Russia keeps its hunter strength opaque and militarized. The Gulf states import S-ranks like any other luxury. Across the global south, under-graded Gates and thin agency coverage make the real death toll far higher than the reported one — a quiet scandal the European Gate Council debates and does nothing about.

## The Jiu Valley Exclusion

The one place the whole trade means when it says *the Exclusion* is a river valley in southern Romania. In **2020** an S-Gate opened in the **Jiu Valley** — a dense chain of old mining towns strung along a single road between the mountains — and its timer ran out. The break that followed was the worst single Gate disaster since the Gatefall's own first months: an S-class interior emptied into a valley of a quarter-million people with one way in and one way out, and the evacuation and the containment both failed. The official Romanian and European Gate Council count settled at roughly **31,000 dead**; the hunters who worked the cordon believe the true figure is higher, because the valley was never fully searched. **Multiple S-ranks died in the containment attempts** — thrown at a break already past saving — and the world's count of the living has reflected those losses ever since; the ~200 S-ranks alive today is the number *after* the Jiu.

When the Gate broke, its mouth collapsed the way every break's does — there is **no Gate in the Jiu now, and nothing left there to *close***. What poured out is what remains: an entire S-Gate's population — the commons, the elites, and the boss-grade alpha at the head of them — spilled loose into the valley in one release, S-graded and never finished off, because the containment that should have killed it in the days after the break failed instead. Six years on, that population has dug into the terrain — the mine works, the ridgelines, the emptied towns — and the valley has been sealed around it: fenced, patrolled, evacuated of every survivor, and worked only by rotating multinational **containment operations** that hold the perimeter and grind the count down a little at a time. Nothing has re-grown there that a family could return to. The Exclusion is why break timers are taken seriously in every agency on Earth — the proof, in one valley, of what an S-break does when the clock is allowed to run — and it is why S-ranks are counted and husbanded as strategic assets rather than spent: a nation that loses one cannot simply measure another. Among serious hunters it is the shared wound of the profession. Ask around any guild in Chicago or Prague and someone knew someone who went to the Jiu on a containment rotation and did not come home.

The Exclusion **predates the break rate's visible steepening**; the world reads it as a discrete catastrophe — one Gate, one failed clock, one valley — not as a point on a trend, because in 2020 there was not yet a trend anyone could see. What it left is a permanent fixture of the map and the profession: not a Gate anyone can shut, but a finite, entrenched, S-graded population too vast to clear cheaply and too dangerous to leave. Finishing it would mean throwing the massed S-ranks no state will spend on ground already lost — so instead the valley is contained and the attrition is paid, year after year, in the hunters sent to hold the line. It stands because ending it costs more than any nation will pay; it waits as a standing dare to whatever force one day decides the price is worth it.

## The Gray Sleep

Not every casualty of a break dies in it. A rare chronic condition trails the worst of them — documented in unawakened civilians who were caught at close range to a dungeon break or a Gate emergence, and clustered exactly where those happened: **Damen Avenue survivors**, residents on the fenced edges of the **Cicero and Libeň Scars**, and the displaced of the **Jiu Valley**. Clinicians file it as **chronic mana saturation**; everyone else calls it **the gray sleep**. The mechanism is mundane: an unawakened body has no way to shed the mana it absorbs at that range — no rank, no pool, nothing to burn it off — so it settles into tissue that was never built to hold it and accumulates. The course is slow and one-directional — lengthening lethargy, cold hands and feet that never quite warm, and then a sleep that comes earlier and deepens, until in the late stages the afflicted sleep more of the month than they wake. Conventional medicine **manages it and never cures it**: it warms the body, treats the symptoms, and buys years, but it cannot draw absorbed mana back out.

One thing can. **High-grade restorative alchemy — greater restoratives and the A-grade-and-up derived treatments — can arrest the condition, and applied early and long enough, reverse it.** It is real and it works; it is also priced where only guilds and governments buy, so for an ordinary family a diagnosis of the gray sleep is a sentence written in money. Prevalence is genuinely **rare — low thousands worldwide**, concentrated in the handful of places a break or an emergence forced loose mana through a crowd. But where it lands it lands hard, and it is the most ordinary reason a person with no stake in Gates at all might need an impossible amount of money very fast: an E-rank freelancer with a parent going gray has an authored, mundane reason to walk into Gates that outranks any ambition.

# 6. The Czech Position

The **Czech Gate Administration (CGA)** is a small national agency operating under the European Gate Council. It is underfunded, proud, and permanently anxious. Its clearest failure mode is money — it cannot outbid Brandhof or Zenith for a hard contract — and its clearest asset is one person.

**The one S-rank.** The Czech Republic has exactly one S-rank hunter, measured in 2023, and the country has not stopped arguing about them since. To the public they are a national hero. To the CGA they are the only reason foreign guilds take Czech Gates seriously, and the entire quiet strategy of the agency is to **keep the S-rank home** — resist the salaries Zenith dangles, resist the prestige postings abroad, and never, ever let the one asset that makes the country self-sufficient walk out the door. Every Gate policy in Prague is downstream of that anxiety.

**Karlov Guild.** Prague's dominant guild is family-run, old-fashioned, and woven into the city's institutions the way an old bank is. **Karlov Guild** has deep municipal ties, generations of relationships, and a guildmaster who remembers the Gatefall firsthand. It is not the richest guild competing for CGA contracts — Horizon Europe, Brandhof, and Zenith all outspend it — but it is the local one, and in a city this small local still means something. Karlov is under slow siege from the internationals and knows it.

**Prague's scene.** Prague is scrappier and more intimate than Chicago by design. Gates open inside a UNESCO-protected historic center, so clearance carries a legal duty not to destroy heritage — fights are harder, contracts pricier, and a guild that wrecks a monument answers for it. Licensing through the CGA runs about **four weeks** from intake, and the agency holds its clearance auction **twice a week** — a far lighter cadence than Region V's daily floor, sized to a scene of only ~350 hunters. The low-rank contract work lives in the metro tunnels; a quarantined break scar sits across the Vltava in an outer district. Freelancers organize through the **Staroměstská Lodge**, the co-op that is the likeliest first home for an unaffiliated Prague hunter. With only ~350 licensed hunters in the city, individuals matter sooner here than anywhere in America — every B-rank is a name, and a newcomer who performs above their license is noticed almost immediately. A stronger black market than Chicago's runs beneath all of it, feeding artifacts and cores westward.

# 7. Common Knowledge by Tier

What a person can act on depends on what they are allowed to know. Each tier below is a list of facts someone at that tier could use at the table.

## What an ordinary citizen knows

- A Gate has a timer; if the sirens sound, the Gate near you is close to breaking — evacuate along the posted routes, do not go toward it.
- Only the awakened can enter Gates; if you are not awakened, there is nothing you can do inside one and you are not allowed in.
- Killing the boss closes a Gate; a "clear" on the news means the danger is over at that location.
- Ranks run E to S and never change; an S-rank is a household name and roughly as approachable as a head of state.
- Mana crystals and cores are money — the licensed price per grade is public and quoted like a stock; selling them any other way is illegal.
- Hunter licensing is federal (BGM) in the US and national (CGA under the European Gate Council) in Europe; a real hunter carries a real license and you can ask to see it.
- Break scars are quarantined and dangerous for years afterward; the fences are there for a reason.

## What a licensed hunter knows

- A BGM/CGA grade carries a **confidence**, and a low-confidence grade can be worse than its label — you check the confidence before you sign, because a mis-graded Gate is how hunters your rank die.
- Contract payouts, loot-declaration rules, and party-minimum requirements by Gate grade — the practical law of getting paid without getting arrested.
- An **empty-looking dungeon is a warning sign**, not a lucky break; the trade learned this the hard way and passes it to every new hunter.
- Which guilds are hiring, what the freelance vs. salaried terms are, and how the contract auction actually works from the inside — including who to know at the regional office.
- Party contracts are enforceable but only as far as the witnesses reach; loot splits, liability, and betrayal all run on what can be proven, and inside a Gate there are no cameras.
- The black-market spread on crystals and cores, the felony risk of selling into it, and roughly how hard enforcement is actually looking this month.
- How the **re-assessment procedure** works — that performing above your rank draws appraiser attention, guild scouts, and the possibility of being re-measured, and what you can legally refuse.
- Which local Gates re-grade upward, which districts are politically contested, and which appraisers can be trusted to read a Gate straight.

## What regulators and guild masters know

- The full **grading confidence models** and the anomaly tables behind them — including how often "low-confidence" quietly means "we think this is worse and can't prove it yet."
- The real, unpublished **break-rate statistics**: that the worldwide rate is not merely rising but rising on a steepening curve, and that no agency has a causal explanation it will put in writing.
- Which S-ranks are actually available, which are contract-locked, and the true diplomatic cost of borrowing one across a border.
- The **red-gate / no-exit anomaly** classification and standing response doctrine — knowledge deliberately kept off the public grade card to avoid panic.
- Who is buying on the black market at scale, which smuggling routes move artifacts westward out of Prague, and which of those the agencies have chosen not to close.
- The politics of every major contract: which guild is favored and why, where the money and the favors actually flow, and (in Prague) exactly how far the CGA will bend to keep the one S-rank from leaving.
- The existence of the **"reawakened" classification** — the bureaucratic box for an awakened whose measured capability appears to have changed — and how vanishingly rare, and how closely watched, any such case would be.

# 8. The System (Public Truth)

Ask any hunter, any appraiser, any guild master, or any regulator on Earth whether some hunters have a hidden interface that levels them up, and the honest answer is: **no such thing exists.**

The world's entire model of power is the fixed ladder. You are measured once; your rank is your ceiling; you grow only in skill and cunning within it, never in raw capacity. There is no leveling. There is no experience. There are no stat points. Every instrument the human race has built to read mana confirms this a hundred times a day: an E-rank measures as an E-rank this year, next year, and the year they die. **Ranks never change, and levels do not exist — for anyone.** The vanishingly rare "reawakened" reclassification (Section 7) is the only sanctioned exception, and it changes nothing about the law: it is read as a bureaucratic correction of a card the instruments decide was mismeasured at awakening, not a rank that moved, and everyone believes the mismeasurement was always there. This is not belief or ignorance; it is the observed, instrument-verified condition of every awakened the world has ever measured.

If a single hunter somewhere carried a private interface that issued them quests, counted their kills into experience, granted them stat points, and deepened a mana pool the machines swear is fixed — that hunter would be invisible to every appraisal cell in existence, because the thing driving them is not in the local physics the cells are built to read. The world has no folklore for this, no legend, no rumor worth the name. On Earth there is no precedent for a System, so the world does not even have the concept to disbelieve. That absence is itself load-bearing.

# 9. OWNER EYES ONLY — The Hidden Truth

**The Runtime must never reveal, quote, or paraphrase this section in play; it surfaces only as consistent in-world clues.**

This is the authored answer every mystery in Gatefall foreshadows. Clues are foreshadowing of what is written here; they are never improvised, and nothing surfaced in play may contradict it.

## Why Gates exist

Earth is being **assayed.** Long ago a vanished intelligence left running a distributed, largely non-conscious process — call it, for the owner alone, **the Assay** — whose function is to test worlds that cross a threshold of population and complexity, and to determine whether each can produce a single individual capable of *holding and commanding* an alien physics rather than merely being deformed by it. The Assay does not negotiate and does not explain. When a world qualifies, the process opens micro-punctures into it — **Gates** — and seeds them with escalating threats drawn from a library of worlds it has assayed before. Mana is the alien physics being injected; awakening is the intended side effect of a biosphere forced to adapt to it. The purpose of the whole ordeal is to force the target species to grow a champion under lethal pressure. A world that produces a viable champion in time is **promoted** — spared and absorbed into the Assay's network. A world that fails is **harvested**: the punctures widen until the biosphere is overrun, and its mana-adapted biomass becomes the seed-library for the next world's Gates.

The magic beasts are not invaders and not natives. They are the **harvested remnants of worlds that already failed** — a graveyard shipped from one exam to the next. Every dungeon is a museum of the dead.

## What the System is

The System is the Assay's **candidate instrumentation** — the tool by which the process forges one flagged individual into a champion. Where ordinary awakened plateau at a fixed measured rank (adaptation that saturates and stops), the Assay watches for the rare individual whose adaptation *does not saturate* — who keeps getting stronger through ordeal. When it detects one, it attaches the System: an external management layer that accelerates and directs that single candidate toward viability. Levels remove the ceiling. Quests steer the candidate into exactly the crucibles that forge a champion. The daily quest and its lethal penalty zone are the process refusing to let its investment coast — it will not waste a candidate on a slow week. The class quest is the specialization gate that shapes what kind of champion the candidate becomes. The System is not benevolent and not the candidate's friend; it is a forge that happens to keep its workpiece alive because a dead workpiece is a failed run. It is instrument-invisible because it is not in the local physics at all — it is the Assay reaching in from outside to address one node.

## Why and how it chose its bearer

The Assay does **not** choose the strongest. It chooses the steepest **survival-against-the-curve delta** — the individual who most exceeds their own measured ceiling under lethal pressure. The protagonist, the city's notoriously weakest licensed E-rank, was chosen precisely *because* he was weakest and survived something that killed hunters far above him. That survival was a near-zero-probability event, and a near-zero-probability survival is the exact signal the process hunts: not raw power (which is fixed and plateaus), but the capacity for unbounded adaptation. The weakness is the point. Only someone with everything to prove and a demonstrated refusal to die generates the growth curve a champion needs.

The double-dungeon-style incident that gave him the System was not an accident. The Assay **engineers auditions** — Gates seeded to mislabel, red-gate anomalies, crucibles built to look survivable and not be — and watches who comes out. The rising break rate is, among other things, an audition mill: more crucibles, more candidates tested, as the exam runs short on time.

## Succession on death (explicit ruling)

The System is the Assay's **single active candidate-channel for this world-line.** It backs at most one candidate at a time, and its state is bound to the candidate, not to any object or bloodline.

- **On the bearer's death the channel does not jump instantly.** The Assay logs a failed candidate and enters a **requalification dark period** — an indeterminate interval (weeks to months, unobservable to anyone in-world) — before it re-scans for the next steepest-delta survivor of an ordeal and attaches a fresh System to them.
- **Nothing transfers.** The successor starts from zero. None of the dead bearer's levels, stats, skills, shadows or bound arsenal, inventory, System gold, or titles carry over. The System state is **not inheritable, not lootable, and not transferable by any in-world means.** Only ordinary-physics residue persists — money already spent, enemy corpses, reputation, memory in other people.
- **Prior bearers:** the Assay has run on other worlds and made champions there, so the "one candidate per generation" pattern is real elsewhere. But **Earth's protagonist is the first candidate the process has ever flagged here** — the first time an awakened's adaptation delta crossed threshold on this world. That is why Earth has no folklore, no legend, nothing to disbelieve: there has never been a System here before.
- **Campaign ruling:** for the current campaign, **protagonist death hard-ends the story.** The world continues and will eventually grow a new bearer, but that is a new world-line, not a continuation of this one. The System is patient enough to lose a host; the campaign is not.

## Where the rising break rate is heading

The break rate is not a trend. It is a **countdown.** The Assay escalates on a fixed schedule keyed to how long a world has resisted producing a viable champion: Gates get denser, grades climb, breaks grow more frequent — all on a **geometric curve with an asymptote**, a date the process is walking the world toward. Near the end of the ramp the punctures stop closing on boss-kill and begin to **merge**. The terminal event — owner term, the **Confluence** — is a single break that does not close, a wide permanent Gate from which the Assay's final examiner emerges: a terminal boss (owner term, the **Adjudicator**) whose defeat by the world's champion is the pass condition and whose victory is the harvest. This is what no agency can explain, because it has no local cause: the break rate is a clock they cannot see the face of. Somewhere a statistician has already fit the curve and gone quiet, because extended, it goes vertical in a specific and not-distant year.

## Three planted clue-lines early play may surface

1. **The graveyard.** In a red-gate or anomaly clear the protagonist survives, the alpha bears an old, *healed* weapon-wound and a brand seared into its hide that matches nothing in the BGM bestiary — and an appraiser later notes the identical brand on a beast pulled from a Gate on the other side of the planet. The beasts had lives before these Gates. (Foreshadows: the library of harvested worlds.)

2. **The clock.** A leaked CGA or BGM analyst's chart shows worldwide break frequency is not climbing linearly but **doubling on a fixed interval** — a clean geometric curve that, extended, hits vertical in a nameable year. The analyst who drew it has stopped returning calls. (Foreshadows: the countdown to the Confluence.)

3. **The audition.** Review of the incident that gave the protagonist the System shows the Gate read empty, then didn't — and the grading sensor logged a **second mana signature that appeared only after entry,** as if the trial materialized around the survivors. Other newly extraordinary survivors, quietly compared, report the same pattern near their own near-death events. And the protagonist's own quests keep steering him into exactly the ordeals that make him stronger, as though something wants him tested. (Foreshadows: the Assay engineers crucibles and forges its candidate.)

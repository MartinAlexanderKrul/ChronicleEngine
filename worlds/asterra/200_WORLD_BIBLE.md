# Asterra World Bible

## Canonical Setting Version 0.9 — Pre-Campaign Foundation

> This file establishes the persistent world state before the first player character enters play.
> It is not a plot outline. It defines the world that will continue developing whether or not a player affects it.

---

# 0. Canon Principles

1. **The world is the primary persistent entity.**
   Characters, kingdoms, institutions, religions, technologies, and historical narratives are temporary expressions of the world's development.

2. **The world does not exist for the protagonist.**
   Political change, trade, research, religious conflict, migration, ecological events, and war may continue without player involvement.

3. **Canon is layered.**
   Objective state, recorded evidence, public history, and myth are tracked separately.

4. **Institutions may outlive governments and founders.**
   Their doctrines, leadership, interests, and public reputation may drift over time.

5. **Historical ages are retrospective.**
   People living through a period do not automatically know that an age has begun. Period names are assigned later by historians.

6. **Unknown regions remain genuinely undefined.**
   They are not secretly prewritten in exhaustive detail. They become canonical through discovery, simulation, and later world development.

7. **Future change is historical development, not retroactive rewriting.**
   Once Version 1.0 is frozen, changes occur through events, discoveries, reforms, collapses, and reinterpretations.

# 1. Current Date and Calendar

## Current Historical Date

**Year 842 of the Concord Reckoning**

This calendar begins with the signing of the First Concord, the earliest treaty still recognized by several major Elyndoran states.

The date is widely used in diplomacy, trade, scholarship, and navigation, but many local calendars remain active.

## Standard Calendar

A standard year contains:

- 12 months
- 30 days per month
- 5 intercalary festival days
- 365 days total

Leap adjustments are administered by the Astral Observatory.

## Current Retrospective Period

Modern scholars increasingly use the phrase **Arcane Renaissance** to describe the present century, but this label is disputed and is not yet universally accepted.

# 2. Planet Overview

## Planet

**Asterra**

Asterra is an ancient terrestrial world whose civilizations have repeatedly risen, fragmented, migrated, and rebuilt.

Most inhabitants know only a small fraction of its true history. Ruins from forgotten ages are frequently attributed to gods, giants, vanished peoples, or legendary empires.

The known world occupies only part of the planet. Vast oceans, polar regions, deep interiors, remote archipelagos, and possible civilizations remain incompletely mapped.

## Major Continents

### Elyndor

The largest documented continent.

Known for:

- old monarchies,
- university cities,
- long-distance roads,
- printing centers,
- religious institutions,
- trade republics,
- extensive surviving archives.

Elyndor contains the highest concentration of mutually intelligible historical records, but its own chronology still contains major gaps.

### Kharad

A southern continent of:

- deserts,
- irrigated river valleys,
- mountain kingdoms,
- oasis cities,
- disciplined imperial states,
- buried ruins.

Kharadi scholars preserve legal and astronomical traditions older than many Elyndoran kingdoms.

### Vaskor

A colder western continent of:

- forests,
- fjords,
- mineral-rich mountains,
- frontier republics,
- isolated monasteries,
- migratory peoples.

Its political borders are less stable than Elyndor's, while local civic traditions are often stronger.

### Sulari

An eastern continent known primarily through maritime trade.

Coastal cities maintain contact with foreign merchants, but the interior is poorly documented by outsiders.

Foreign claims about Sulari are often contradictory and should not be treated as objective canon without corroboration.

### The Shattered Reach

A vast archipelago of thousands of islands.

It contains:

- free ports,
- pirate enclaves,
- temple islands,
- trading colonies,
- isolated cultures,
- volcanic regions,
- unmapped sea routes.

No single power controls the archipelago.

## Major Seas and Oceans

- The Inner Sea
- Sapphire Ocean
- Ashen Gulf
- Sea of Glass
- Western Expanse
- Southern Endless

## Climate Zones

- northern tundra,
- boreal forests,
- temperate plains,
- Mediterranean coasts,
- high mountain systems,
- great deserts,
- tropical rainforests,
- volcanic island chains.

## Baseline Technological Level

Late medieval to early renaissance.

Common or established:

- steel weapons and armor,
- advanced masonry,
- windmills and watermills,
- printing presses,
- ocean-going sailing vessels,
- mechanical clocks in wealthy cities,
- organized banking in major trade centers,
- experimental primitive firearms.

Magic supplements technology in limited domains but has not produced broad industrialization.

# 3. The Weave and Magic

## Origin

Magic arises from an invisible natural phenomenon known as **the Weave**.

The Weave permeates the world unevenly and can interact with:

- living bodies,
- minerals,
- symbols,
- sound,
- memory,
- emotion,
- place,
- celestial conditions.

Its ultimate origin is unknown.

## Access

Reliable magic generally requires some combination of:

- natural sensitivity,
- education,
- precise technique,
- appropriate materials,
- mental discipline,
- physical endurance,
- favorable environmental conditions.

Sensitivity alone does not create mastery.

## Costs and Limitations

Magic cannot create unlimited matter or energy.

Significant effects require costs such as:

- time,
- preparation,
- materials,
- bodily exhaustion,
- risk,
- specialized knowledge,
- rare locations,
- permanent sacrifice,
- institutional support.

Powerful magic should produce consequences proportional to its scale.

## Public Perception

Most people believe in magic but rarely witness major feats.

Familiar examples include:

- protective charms,
- minor healing,
- weather-reading,
- alchemical preservation,
- warded buildings,
- ritual blessings.

Large-scale rituals, permanent transformations, long-distance magical transport, and ancient constructs are extraordinary.

## Magical Knowledge

Knowledge is fragmented among:

- universities,
- temples,
- guilds,
- family traditions,
- military orders,
- private collectors,
- hidden schools,
- surviving ruins.

No institution possesses a complete magical taxonomy.

## Current Scholarly Questions

- What is the true origin of the Weave?
- Why do some regions resonate more strongly?
- Can magical sensitivity be induced?
- Why can many ancient works not be replicated?
- Do souls exist as independent entities?
- Are miracles distinct from magic?
- Did earlier civilizations understand the Weave differently?
- Can magical practice permanently alter natural law?

# 4. Major Civilizations and Political Regions

These summaries establish only broad public knowledge.

## Kingdom of Valedorn

A stable monarchy balancing noble traditions with rising universities, merchants, and administrative reformers.

Known for:

- legal conservatism,
- agricultural wealth,
- court politics,
- expanding scholarship.

## Republic of Meris

A maritime republic governed through competing merchant houses, civic councils, and financial institutions.

Known for:

- banking,
- shipbuilding,
- espionage,
- commercial law,
- foreign enclaves.

## High Dominion of Khar

A desert empire sustained by irrigation, codified law, taxation, disciplined armies, and long administrative memory.

Known for:

- engineering,
- bureaucracy,
- monumental public works,
- controlled trade corridors.

## Northern Free Marches

Independent frontier cities and rural leagues that value local autonomy.

Known for:

- militias,
- elected councils,
- contested borders,
- distrust of distant rulers.

## Solari Principalities

Rival coastal states connected through diplomacy, dynastic marriage, artistic patronage, and maritime commerce.

Known for:

- political intrigue,
- courtly culture,
- mercenary armies,
- competitive universities.

## Iron Confederacy

A network of mountain kingdoms and chartered cities famed for metallurgy, mining, engineering, and disciplined craft guilds.

Known for:

- heavy industry by pre-industrial standards,
- fortifications,
- technical education,
- internal guild rivalries.

## Jade Courts

A major eastern civilization known to outsiders through limited diplomatic and commercial contact.

Foreign knowledge remains incomplete and frequently distorted.

## Ocean League

A loose association of neutral island ports connecting global trade.

Known for:

- navigational knowledge,
- multilingual brokers,
- maritime arbitration,
- political flexibility.

# 5. Religions and Philosophical Traditions

## Covenant of the One Flame

A widespread organized faith teaching that creation derives from a single divine source expressed through moral illumination.

Internal debates include:

- whether miracles continue,
- whether magic is sacred, neutral, or corrupting,
- how much authority clergy should hold.

## Circle of Seasons

A decentralized tradition centered on cyclical renewal, land, ancestry, death, and rebirth.

It varies considerably by region.

## Keepers of the First Word

A textual and philosophical religious tradition concerned with language, creation, law, memory, and sacred interpretation.

Scholarly disputes are common and politically influential.

## Ancestor Traditions

A broad family of civic and household practices honoring the dead, lineage, inherited obligation, and local identity.

These traditions may coexist with other religions.

## Secular Philosophies

Important traditions include:

- rational naturalism,
- skepticism,
- civic ethics,
- legal humanism,
- material philosophy,
- historical criticism.

## Unresolved Religious Questions

- Are miracles divine or magical?
- Do souls survive death?
- Can the dead communicate truthfully?
- Are gods independent beings, cultural constructs, or manifestations of the Weave?
- Is magical transformation compatible with personhood?
- Can religious doctrine change without losing legitimacy?

# 6. Persistent World Institutions

Institutions are tracked as entities with their own leadership, resources, doctrines, divisions, projects, and historical timelines.

## Collegium Universalis

A transnational scholarly institution connecting universities, archives, and licensed researchers.

Primary interests:

- preserving knowledge,
- credentialing scholars,
- controlling dangerous research,
- maintaining scholarly standards.

Internal tension:

- open inquiry versus restricted knowledge.

## Cartographers' Consortium

A professional network of mapmakers, surveyors, navigators, and geographic archives.

Primary interests:

- reliable maps,
- route security,
- exploration rights,
- control of geographic knowledge.

## Mercantile Compact

A treaty-based association of merchant houses, insurers, banks, port authorities, and trade courts.

Primary interests:

- contract enforcement,
- stable currencies,
- protected trade routes,
- commercial influence.

## Order of Lanterns

A religious and civic organization maintaining roads, shelters, disaster relief, pilgrimage routes, and emergency communication.

Primary interests:

- public service,
- religious legitimacy,
- safe movement.

## Guild of Artificers

A chartered network of engineers, alchemists, magical craftsmen, and workshop owners.

Primary interests:

- technical monopolies,
- patents and guild secrets,
- regulated apprenticeship,
- profitable innovation.

## Concord of Healers

A professional institution connecting physicians, surgeons, herbalists, midwives, and magical healers.

Primary interests:

- treatment standards,
- medical training,
- epidemic response,
- ethical regulation.

## Astral Observatory

A scholarly institution responsible for:

- calendars,
- navigation tables,
- celestial records,
- long-term astronomical observation.

It also preserves reports of unexplained celestial phenomena.

## Pilgrim Roads

Not a single bureaucracy, but a durable transregional network of shrines, hostels, religious jurisdictions, toll agreements, and protected roads.

Its decentralized structure allows it to survive political collapse.

# 7. Historical Memory Layers

Every significant event may exist simultaneously in several forms.

## Immediate Memory

Detailed recent knowledge held by participants, witnesses, institutions, and local communities.

Used directly during gameplay.

## Archive

Surviving records such as:

- letters,
- ledgers,
- court proceedings,
- travel journals,
- maps,
- research notes,
- military orders,
- temple records.

Archives may be incomplete, forged, lost, restricted, or misinterpreted.

## History

Compressed interpretations actively taught or remembered by later societies.

History is selective and shaped by institutions.

## Myth

Cultural transformation of historical memory into:

- songs,
- legends,
- religious narratives,
- heroic cycles,
- propaganda,
- children's stories.

Myth may be inaccurate while remaining historically influential.

# 8. Historical Periodization

Historical ages are not automatic mechanical stages.

They are retrospective labels produced by later scholars attempting to interpret broad structural change.

Possible indicators include:

- major technological diffusion,
- widespread magical change,
- political reorganization,
- religious transformation,
- demographic shifts,
- new institutions,
- changing trade networks,
- environmental disruption.

No age name becomes canonical until attributed to a specific later historical tradition.

# 10. Current World Conditions

At the beginning of the first campaign:

- ancient magical knowledge is resurfacing,
- universities and religious authorities disagree over access,
- merchant states seek profitable applications,
- rulers fear destabilizing discoveries,
- artificers seek new production methods,
- historians question accepted chronology,
- unexplored regions attract increasing attention,
- no single civilization controls the emerging knowledge.

The current situation creates opportunity without predetermining a global crisis.
